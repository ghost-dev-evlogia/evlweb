## Part 1 — Voice analysis (what makes dbrand's copy work)

**Tone:** deadpan, aggressive, self-aware, and gleefully hostile — it insults the customer, its
competitors, and itself, as a bit. It treats a phone case like a military-grade weapons program. The
confidence is the product. Where PostHog is *warm* irreverence, dbrand is *cold* irreverence — pick the
temperature deliberately; don't blend them into mush.

**Signature moves:**
- **Military/industrial framing of mundane products.** "PROJECT KILLSWITCH", "Tank", "Ghost", "Prism",
  "Darkplates" — product names as codenames, not descriptors. A screen protector becomes armor.
- **Terse, absolute taglines.** "Ghost Case 2.0 — The next-gen clear case." No hedging, no feature list
  in the headline.
- **Relentless, self-parodying urgency.** The sale marquee scrolls forever: *"SUMMER SALE · UP TO $55 OFF
  KILLSWITCH · SUMMER SALE · 30% OFF LEATHER · SUMMER SALE · $20 OFF GRIP CASE + SCREEN PROTECTOR BUNDLE
  …"* — the repetition itself is the joke and the pressure.
- **Deadpan product-as-artifact framing.** The "Manifesto" is sold as a $75 physical artifact:
  "Buy This Artifact - $75.00 / Free Steel Bookmark / Free Global Shipping."
- **Limited editions with punchline names.** "Touch Grass", "Circuit Board", "Sketch 2D".
- **Extreme configurability presented flatly.** Long device lists (every iPhone/Galaxy/Pixel) with zero
  hand-holding — respects the buyer's intelligence.

**Lesson for Evlogia's farm voice:** dbrand proves a B2C brand can be *aggressively* itself and win. For
Evlogia (B2B, serious buyers) borrow the **confidence and the codename-framing** (a "field", a "harvest",
a "greenhouse" as deliberate product-area names), and the **deadpan absolute taglines** — but keep the
hostility dialled way down. Warmth (PostHog) fits the cozy farm better than dbrand's coldness; use dbrand
as the reference for *nerve*, PostHog as the reference for *tone*.
